Styles of Citation
There are several styles and forms used to cite sources which support research papers. Most academic institutions have established standardized requirements for a preferred style. Three of the most widely used styles of citation are:
    Modern Language Association (MLA)
    Chicago Manual of Style
    American Psychological Association (APA)

Whatever style you are using, accuracy, clarity, and consistency are the most important factors to consider when citing various types of sources. Guidelines for citing electronic and on-line sources are not yet standardized in most citation styles, as the constantly-changing nature of electronic databases and sources on the internet requires citation guidelines to be flexible in order to maintain their usefulness.

In general, most styles require that you not only identify reference sources within the text of your essay, but also provide a separate list of works used in your research.
Modern Language Association Top of Page

The Modern Language Association (MLA) is an organization of teachers and scholars devoted to the study of language and literature. MLA style has been widely adopted by academic journals, schools, and instructors. Since its initial publication, the MLA Style Manual has become the predominant style guide for use in the Humanities in the United States, and is commonly used in Canada and other countries worldwide.

The MLA Style Guide offers extensive standardized methods of citing sources for research, including electronic sources. MLA style calls for the use of in-text citations and a list of sources at the end of the text, called Works Cited.

In-text citation format:
As Morris Dickstein claims, Steinbeck’s depiction of the Joad family aroused such sympathy simply because it had the fee of journalistic accuracy (112), brought about by “countless hours of talking to migrant people, working beside them, listening to them, and sharing their problems” (DeMott 170).
Basic works cited format:
deMott, Robert. Steinbeck’s Typewriter: Essays on His Art. Troy, NY: SJK
         Publishing Industries, Inc. 1996. Literary Reference Center Plus. Web. 3 Mar.
         2010.
    Halverson, Cathryn. "John Steinbeck's Sweetheart: The Cosmic American Bus"
      College Literature. 35.1(2008): 82-89. Literary Reference Center. Web.
      3 Mar. 2010.

These are basic examples of the MLA style for electronic references. For more information and examples of electronic reference formats recommended by the Modern Language Association, see the MLA Handbook for Writers of Research Papers - Seventh Edition (2009) or the MLA Style Manual and Guide to Scholarly Publishing - Third Edition (2008). Basic information on MLA Style is available at http://www.mla.org/style.

Chicago Style Top of Page

Begun in the 1890s as a simple list of style rules jotted down on a sheet of paper by a proofreader at the University of Chicago Press, this style guide was expanded and published in book format in 1906, as A Manual of Style. Since then, the Chicago Manual of Style has become a highly regarded and widely used resource for writers and editors in the publishing field.

The Chicago Manual offers two styles of documentation, each one favored by different groups of scholars. The author-date style is widely used by those in the natural or social science fields, while the humanities or documentary-note style is favored by those in literature, history and the arts. The humanities style (shown below) uses notes for citations and includes a bibliography.

Chicago style calls for the use of footnotes or endnotes. Material from research sources within your essay should be numbered, and notes with corresponding numbers should be placed at either the bottom of the page for footnotes or at the end of the essay for endnotes.

Basic footnote format:

As Morris Dickstein claims, Steinbeck’s depiction of the Joad family aroused such sympathy simply because it had the fee of journalistic accuracy1, brought about by “countless hours of talking to migrant people, working beside them, listening to them, and sharing their problems”2 .

      1. Morris Dickstein, “Steinbeck and the Great Depression,” South
      Atlantic Quarterly 103, no. 1 (2004): 111-131,
      http://search.epnet.com. (Accessed August 24, 2005).

      2. Robert deMott. Steinbeck’s Typewriter: Essays on His Art (Troy,
             NY: SJK Publishing Industries, 1996).

Basic bibliography entry format:

    deMott, Robert. Steinbeck’s Typewriter: Essays on His Art. (Troy, NY: Whitston, 1996).
    Dickstein, Morris. “Steinbeck and the Great Depression” South Atlantic Quarterly 103,
      no. 1 (2004): 111-131, http://search.epnet.com. (Accessed August 24, 2005).
These are basic examples of the Chicago style. For more information, see the Chicago Manual of Style (15th edition). For more information on Chicago Style, see http://www.press.uchicago.edu/Misc/Chicago/cmosfaq/.

American Psychological Association Top of Page
Founded in 1892 by 26 men, the American Psychological Association (APA) has since expanded to include roughly 150,000 members and 53 professional divisions.

The main scholarly association for academic psychologists in the United States, the APA has also developed standardized methods for citing print and electronic sources used in research. APA Style calls for the use of in-text citation, giving the author’s last name, the source’s publication year and a page reference, and a list of sources at the end of the text, called References.

In-text citation format:
As Morris Dickstein claims, Steinbeck’s depiction of the Joad family aroused such sympathy simply because it had the fee of journalistic accuracy (2004, p. 112), brought about by “countless hours of talking to migrant people, working beside them, listening to them, and sharing their problems” (DeMott, 1996, p. 170).
Basic reference format:
deMott, R. (1996). Steinbeck’s Typewriter: Essays on His Art. Troy, NY: Whitston.
    Dickstein, M. (2004). Steinbeck and the Great Depression. South Atlantic Quarterly, 103,
      111-191. Retrieved March 5, 2010, from Literary Reference Center Database.

The examples shown above are basic examples of the APA style. For more information, see the Publication Manual of the American Psychological Association (5th Ed.). For more information on electronic reference formats recommended by the American Psychological Association, see http://www.apastyle.org/elecref.html.
